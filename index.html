Alex Wallar


I am a student contractor in the Distributed Autonomous Systems Group at the Naval Research Laboratory conducting research in information theoretic surveillance for multi-agent systems. I received a First Class Honours degree in computer science from the University of St Andrews in Scotland. Starting in September 2015, I will be a PhD candidate in the Distributed Robotics Laboratory at MIT developing algorithms for autonomous surveillance and persistent monitoring.

Rover

Risk sensitive surveillance using unmanned aerial vehicles

By using simple local rules, we created an algorithm that can provide risk sensitive surveillance over a given area using a team of quadrotors. The algorithm reduces the frequency of surveillance of risky areas and increases its altitude whilst being aware of its sensor quality to avoid the risk. See these papers for more information [1, 2]

dCRoPS

Path planning for swarms in dynamic environments

We have developed a novel path planning algorithm for swarms that uses probabilistic roadmaps for global planning and potential fields for local planning. The algorithm is able to plan the movements of a swarm around static and dynamic obstacles in order to reach the goal. See these papers for information [1, 2]

Malt

Multi-agent source localization

Malt is a multi-agent source localization toolkit that is used to determine the location of undirected stimuli by combining the knowledge from each of the agents. The agents move around the area in order reduce the noise in their sensor readings to better predict the location of the source.

Racer

Path planning in dynamic environments by predicting the trajectory of moving obstacles

Racer is a project that I am working on as part of my senior honours project. This algorithm uses the previous movements of dynamic obstacles to build a Markov model of future trajectories that the obstacle will follow. It will use combine this knowledge with multiple planning techniques to create safer, collision free paths for an agent even when the dynamic obstacles are much larger and faster than the agent.
